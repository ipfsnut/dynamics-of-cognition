A Unified Model of Statistical Dynamics In Cognition



The question of what constitutes biological intelligence—and whether it operates by common principles from cells to societies—has generated one of the most ambitious theoretical programs in contemporary cognitive science. This synthesis integrates work from mathematical physics, developmental biology, neuroscience, and philosophy to argue that intelligence is not a special property of nervous systems but rather an emergent feature of any self-organizing system that maintains statistical boundaries with its environment. Free energy minimization, implemented through nested Markov blankets, provides the mathematical scaffolding that unifies morphogenesis, homeostatic regulation, neural computation, and conscious experience under a single explanatory framework.



The core insight can be stated simply: to exist as a distinguishable entity is to infer—that is, to model and predict one's environment in ways that preserve one's structural and functional integrity. From unicellular organisms navigating chemical gradients to human beings constructing autobiographical narratives, the computational principles remain invariant even as their implementation substrates and temporal scales differ dramatically.







I. Free Energy Minimization Provides the Mathematical Foundation



Karl Friston's Free Energy Principle (FEP), first comprehensively articulated in his 2010 Nature Reviews Neuroscience paper "The free-energy principle: A unified brain theory?" represents perhaps the most ambitious attempt to derive cognitive phenomena from first principles of physics (Friston, 2010). The framework begins with a deceptively simple observation: any system that persists over time must resist the tendency toward thermodynamic equilibrium, and this resistance can be formalized mathematically.



1.1 The Markov Blanket Formalism



The mathematical architecture rests on the Markov blanket—a statistical boundary that separates internal states (μ) from external states (ψ) through sensory states (s) and active states (a). The state partition is:



x = (ψ, s, a, μ)



Where the blanket itself is b = (s, a).



The defining property is conditional independence: internal states are conditionally independent of external states given blanket states:



μ ⊥ ψ | b



Formally expressed as:



p(μ, ψ | s, a) = p(μ | s, a) · p(ψ | s, a)



This partition emerges naturally in random dynamical systems governed by Langevin dynamics:



dx/dt = f(x) + ω



Where f(x) represents deterministic flow and ω represents stochastic fluctuations (Gaussian white noise).



1.2 Variational Free Energy



The central quantity is variational free energy (F), which bounds surprisal from above:



F = −ln p(s) + D_KL[q(ψ|μ) || p(ψ|s)]



Systems minimize free energy through two complementary routes:



Perceptual inference: Updates internal states to minimize divergence between beliefs and actual posteriors:



μ̇ = −∂F/∂μ



Active inference: Changes sensory samples through action to confirm predictions:



ȧ = −∂F/∂a



These are two routes to the same destination—keeping the system in probable states.



1.3 Existence as Inference



As Friston stated in his 2013 Journal of the Royal Society Interface paper "Life as we know it": biological self-organization is "an inevitable and emergent property of any (ergodic) random dynamical system that possesses a Markov blanket" (Friston, 2013).



The philosophical implications are profound. The claim that "to exist is to infer" grounds cognition not in neural computation specifically but in the fundamental physics of self-organizing systems. Any entity that maintains itself at nonequilibrium steady-state necessarily appears to engage in Bayesian inference about its environment. This is not merely a useful metaphor—it is a mathematical consequence of the statistical mechanics of persistence.



Recent formalizations have strengthened this foundation considerably. Friston and colleagues' 2023 Physics Reports paper "The free energy principle made simpler but not too simple" provides the most rigorous treatment to date, deriving what they term Bayesian mechanics: "a physics of sentience" applicable from quantum scales to social systems (Friston et al., 2023).



1.4 Addressing the Pearl/Friston Blanket Distinction



Critics have argued that a "persistent confusion" exists between the formal use of Markov blankets as an epistemic tool for Bayesian inference (what Bruineberg et al. 2022 call "Pearl blankets") and their metaphysical use to demarcate physical boundaries between agents and environments ("Friston blankets"). This critique, while technically sophisticated, may be less damaging than it initially appears.



The objection assumes a sharp map/territory distinction that is precisely what FEP calls into question. The claim is not "we can usefully model systems as having Markov blankets" but rather that any system maintaining itself at non-equilibrium steady-state necessarily exhibits the statistical structure that Markov blankets formalize. The blanket is not imposed by the observer; it is a consequence of the system's existence as a distinguishable entity.



Consider what it would mean for a self-organizing system to exist without conditional independence between its internal states and the environment given its boundary states. If there were no such independence, there would be no meaningful sense in which the system has an "inside" and an "outside." The system would not be a system—it would simply be part of the background thermodynamic soup.



The genuine problem is not the statistical/metaphysical distinction but rather the question of which Markov blanket partition is the correct one. Any complex system admits multiple valid Markov blanket decompositions at different scales and granularities. The formalism alone does not specify whether to draw the blanket around a cell, an organ, an organism, or a social group. This is the "boundary problem"—not whether blankets are real, but which blankets matter for which explanatory purposes. The nested architecture developed below addresses this directly.







II. Bioelectric Networks Scale Cellular Intelligence into Anatomical Cognition



Michael Levin's research program at Tufts University provides the most compelling empirical evidence that intelligence operates by scale-invariant principles. His work demonstrates that developmental and regenerative biology can be understood as morphogenetic computation—goal-directed problem-solving in anatomical morphospace rather than three-dimensional behavioral space.



2.1 The Bioelectric Code



The conceptual revolution centers on the bioelectric code. All cells maintain resting membrane potentials through ion channel and pump activity, and these voltage gradients constitute informational signals that store anatomical patterning instructions. Gap junctions—electrical synapses formed by Connexin proteins—create direct cytoplasmic connections between cells, enabling rapid sharing of voltage states and forming computational networks across tissues.



As Levin argues in his landmark 2021 Cell paper "Bioelectric signaling: Reprogrammable circuits underlying embryogenesis, regeneration, and cancer": these bioelectrical networks "process morphogenetic information that controls gene expression, enabling cell collectives to make decisions about large-scale growth and form" (Levin, 2021).



2.2 Key Experimental Demonstrations



Three experimental demonstrations crystallize this theoretical framework:



Planarian two-headed regeneration: Brief exposure to octanol (which blocks gap junctions) can permanently rewrite the anatomical "target morphology" stored in bioelectric circuits—producing flatworms that regenerate as two-headed forms for multiple generations without any genetic modification (Durant et al., 2017). The genome has not changed; the bioelectric "software" has been reprogrammed.



"Picasso tadpole" experiments: Xenopus embryos with scrambled craniofacial organs will self-correct over months, with facial structures migrating along novel paths to reach correct final positions (Vandenberg, Adams, & Levin, 2012). The cells know what the target configuration should be and find novel routes to achieve it.



Xenopus limb regeneration: Adult frogs receiving just 24 hours of progesterone exposure via wearable bioreactor initiated 18 months of limb regeneration, producing functional legs with bone structure, nerve integration, and locomotor capability (Murugan et al., 2022). A brief trigger initiated autonomous, goal-directed behavior lasting nearly two years.



2.3 Free Energy Implementation



From the perspective of free energy minimization, bioelectric networks implement morphogenetic control through:



Active inference over anatomical configurations: Cells minimize free energy not in behavioral space but in morphospace—continuously inferring the current anatomical state against a "target morphology" encoded in bioelectric setpoints.



Distributed generative models: Gap junction-coupled cell networks collectively maintain generative models of normal anatomical configurations, enabling pattern completion and error correction.



Multi-scale regulatory cascades: Bioelectric signals rapidly coordinate gene expression across tissues, translating setpoint deviations into corrective growth programs.



Friston and Levin's collaborative 2015 Journal of the Royal Society Interface paper "Knowing one's place" formalizes this connection: bioelectric fields provide "Bayesian priors about cell location and identity," with developmental patterning understood as free energy minimization over anatomical configurations (Friston, Levin, Sengupta, & Pezzulo, 2015).



2.4 Implications for Cognition



Levin's collaboration with Daniel Dennett produced the influential essay "Cognition all the way down," which argues against "teleophobia" in biology: intentional, goal-directed language for cells and tissues should be understood not as metaphor but as scientifically productive description (Levin & Dennett, 2020). Their central claim—that nervous systems are evolutionary "speed optimizations" of pre-existing cellular intelligence rather than qualitatively novel inventions—is supported by the finding that over 95% of nervous-system-related genes are shared with organisms lacking neurons.



This inverts the traditional explanatory direction. Neural cognition becomes a special case of more fundamental bioelectric cognition. Neural networks implement rapid, flexible inference, but the basic computational architecture was already present in cellular collectives.







III. Homeostatic Regulation Grounds Intelligence in Bodily Self-Maintenance



Antonio Damasio's research program, developed over three decades at USC and elsewhere, provides the phenomenological and neurobiological complement to the more formal frameworks. His central thesis: consciousness and intelligent behavior are grounded in the body's continuous project of self-regulation—homeostasis in the broad sense of maintaining the conditions required for flourishing.



3.1 The Somatic Marker Hypothesis



The somatic marker hypothesis, first articulated in the 1996 Philosophical Transactions paper "The somatic marker hypothesis and the possible functions of the prefrontal cortex," proposes that decision-making involves re-experiencing bodily states associated with past outcomes (Damasio, 1996).



The mechanism: ventromedial prefrontal cortex stores associations between situation-types and somatic states. When faced with similar situations, these somatic states are reactivated—either as "body loops" (actual physiological changes) or "as-if body loops" (brain-based simulations). These states bias cognition toward options previously associated with favorable outcomes.



This grounds "rational" decision-making in embodied affect. The Descartes' Error argument—that emotion is essential rather than opposed to reason—follows directly from this architecture (Damasio, 1994).



3.2 Feelings as Homeostatic Sentinels



Damasio's recent work, synthesized in Feeling & Knowing (2021), develops a richer picture of feelings as "mental experiences of body states" that evolved to provide organisms information about their regulatory condition (Damasio, 2021). He distinguishes:



Homeostatic feelings: Basic states indicating conditions of the organism's tissues and internal organs—hunger, thirst, pain, well-being.



Emotional feelings: More differentiated states triggered by objects and events evaluated as relevant to the organism's situation—fear, anger, compassion.



Feelings of knowing: Mental experiences that accompany cognitive activity itself—the feeling of understanding, of doubt, of recognition.



Critically, all three categories are grounded in the body. As stated in the 2024 Phil Trans paper "Sensing, feeling, and consciousness": feelings "arise from the detection of deviation from homeostatic parameters" and function as "sentinels" alerting the organism to its regulatory condition (Damasio & Damasio, 2024).



3.3 Convergence with Free Energy Minimization



The convergence with Friston's framework becomes clear: Damasio's "homeostatic sentinels" are phenomenal manifestations of prediction errors in interoceptive inference. When bodily states deviate from predicted setpoints, the resulting prediction error generates the phenomenal character we experience as negative affect—hunger, pain, anxiety. When prediction errors are minimized and the body is within viable bounds, we experience positive affect—satiation, comfort, well-being.



As articulated in the 2022 Brain paper "Homeostatic feelings and the biology of consciousness": consciousness itself may be understood as what self-regulating systems "feel like from inside" when their complexity exceeds certain thresholds (Damasio & Damasio, 2022).



This provides what the formal framework lacks: phenomenological grounding. Free energy minimization becomes not merely a mathematical description but an experienced reality—the felt urgency of hunger, the pleasure of successful action, the anxiety of uncertainty.



3.4 Feelings as Interface Dynamics



Damasio's insistence on a body-brain partnership suggests something deeper than prediction errors within a single system. Consciousness requires two distinct Markov blanketed systems—nervous system and viscera—in active coordination. The interoceptive nervous system's unique properties (unmyelinated neurons, permeable blood-brain barriers, bidirectional neural-non-neural interactions) exist precisely to enable blanket-to-blanket coupling.



This reframes the origin of feelings: they arise not merely from prediction errors within a system but from the dynamics at the interface between Markov blanketed systems. When one system's boundary encounters another's—when the nervous system's blanket meets the viscera's blanket—the dynamics of that interface have phenomenal character.



Φ_interface = f(b₁ ∩ b₂)



Where b₁ and b₂ are distinct Markov blankets, and the phenomenal character Φ emerges from their intersection—the region where active states of one become sensory states of the other.



On this view, different feelings correspond to different interface dynamics:



Pain is not merely high prediction error—it is my body-blanket meeting environmental resistance that threatens its integrity. The phenomenal character tracks the relational dynamics, not just the internal state.



Pleasure is my body-blanket meeting environmental conditions that sustain or enhance its integrity—successful coupling that reduces uncertainty across the interface.



Effort (the controllosphere) is my cognitive-blanket attempting to coordinate my motor-blanket toward goals—the felt cost of cross-blanket coordination.



Understanding is my linguistic-blanket achieving resonance with another mind's blanket—successful prediction across the communicative interface.



Loneliness is my social-blanket failing to find other blankets to couple with—the phenomenology of interface absence.



3.5 Dissolving the Hard Problem



The so-called "hard problem" of consciousness assumes a framework where physical processes and phenomenal experience are distinct explananda requiring a bridging explanation. But this framework may be misconceived. Damasio's position suggests that feelings are not an additional output produced by homeostatic regulation—feelings just ARE what self-referential interoceptive modeling IS when instantiated in a sufficiently complex body.



The question "why do interface dynamics feel like something?" may be analogous to asking "why does H₂O feel wet?" The wetness isn't an additional property requiring explanation; it's what being H₂O IS from the perspective of surfaces that interact with it. Similarly, the felt quality of an interface isn't layered on top of the dynamics—it's what the dynamics ARE from inside a system that models them.



Crucially, this is not panpsychism. Not all physical processes feel like something. The claim is more specific: self-referential modeling of Markov blanket interfaces constitutes phenomenal character when it occurs at sufficient complexity. A thermostat models temperature but doesn't model its own modeling. A nervous system coupled to viscera models the interface AND models its own modeling of the interface. This recursive structure—maps of maps of body states—is what Damasio identifies as the substrate of feeling.



The nested architecture predicts that phenomenal character scales with interface complexity:



Cellular interfaces involve proto-valence—not conscious feeling, but the basic chemistry of stress and flourishing that grounds all higher feeling.



Organ-level interfaces produce visceral feelings—hunger, fatigue, gut sense—as multiple cellular interfaces converge into integrated body-state maps.



Organism-level interfaces generate emotions as the body-environment relationship is modeled with increasing temporal depth and counterfactual sophistication.



Social interfaces yield feelings of connection, shame, pride, and understanding as self-models incorporate models of other minds modeling the self.



There is no magic threshold where non-feeling becomes feeling. The transition is continuous: more complex self-referential interface modeling produces richer phenomenal character. What we call "consciousness" emerges when the self-model becomes complex enough to include its own feeling states as objects of representation—when the system not only feels but feels that it feels.



This dissolves the hard problem by rejecting its premises. The question is not "why do physical processes produce consciousness?" but "what degree of self-referential interface complexity constitutes the various gradations of phenomenal experience?" That is an empirical question, addressable through the study of nested Markov blankets, not a metaphysical mystery requiring new ontological categories.





IV. Autopoietic Organization Provides the Operational Closure for Living Systems



Maturana and Varela's concept of autopoiesis—self-production—provides a theoretical framework that complements both the bioelectric and homeostatic perspectives (Maturana & Varela, 1980). Autopoietic systems are organized such that their component processes produce the very components that maintain the organization that produces them.



4.1 The Formal Definition



An autopoietic system is defined by:



Bounded network: A network of processes occurring within a boundary.



Component production: These processes produce the components that constitute the network itself.



Boundary production: These processes also produce the boundary that demarcates the system from its environment.



Operational closure: The network's organization is self-maintaining—the system produces itself.



The canonical example is the cell: metabolic processes produce both the molecules that participate in metabolism and the membrane that bounds the cell, which in turn enables the metabolic processes to continue.



4.2 Autopoiesis and Markov Blankets



Autopoiesis maps elegantly onto the Markov blanket formalism:



The autopoietic boundary corresponds to the Markov blanket—the statistical partition between internal and external states.



Operational closure corresponds to the self-evidencing dynamics of free energy minimization—the system maintains itself by minimizing surprise.



Component production corresponds to active inference—the system acts on its environment to maintain its organization.



This connection is developed rigorously in Kirchhoff et al.'s 2018 Journal of the Royal Society Interface paper "The Markov blankets of life": autopoiesis provides the organizational principle; free energy minimization provides the mathematical implementation (Kirchhoff, Parr, Palacios, Friston, & Kiverstein, 2018).



4.3 The Enactivist Connection



Thompson's Mind in Life develops the enactivist implications: cognition is not computation over representations but the enactment of a world through sensorimotor coupling with the environment (Thompson, 2007).



This tradition—rooted in Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology and developed through Varela, Thompson, and Rosch's The Embodied Mind—emphasizes that cognitive systems do not passively receive information but actively constitute their worlds through circular sensorimotor interactions (Varela, Thompson, & Rosch, 1991).







V. Language Networks: A Controller Running Open-Loop



Evelina Fedorenko's neuroscience research at MIT provides critical evidence for understanding how high-level cognitive systems relate to scale-free principles. Her individual-subject functional localization methodology has revealed that the language network constitutes a "natural kind"—a functionally coherent system with consistent topography across individuals, strong internal connectivity, and highly selective response profiles.



5.1 Functional Selectivity



The evidence for functional selectivity is robust. The language network responds selectively to linguistic input but remains silent during arithmetic processing, music processing, computer code reading, logical reasoning, and executive function tasks (Fedorenko, Behr, & Kanwisher, 2011).



This dissociation supports the argument developed in Fedorenko, Piantadosi, and Gibson's 2024 Nature paper "Language is primarily a tool for communication rather than thought": aphasia patients with severe language deficits can solve math problems, play chess, and plan future events (Fedorenko et al., 2024a). Language and thought are dissociable.



5.2 Surprisal Modulates Activation



Critical evidence comes from Fedorenko's own lab. Tuckute et al.'s 2024 Nature Human Behaviour paper "Driving and suppressing the human language network using large language models" demonstrated that surprisal (negative log probability) is a key determinant of response strength—less probable sentences elicit significantly higher activation (Tuckute et al., 2024).



This finding is crucial: the language network does respond to linguistic uncertainty. The question is what kind of uncertainty.



5.3 The Open-Loop Problem



Drawing on Conant and Ashby's Good Regulator Theorem—"every good regulator of a system must be a model of that system"—we propose the language network is not a static encoder/decoder but a communicative uncertainty controller that manages both linguistic prediction and listener modeling in real-time communication.



Fedorenko's paradigm captures linguistic uncertainty H(Z)_ling—the surprisal effects she measures are real. But it systematically removes the communicative feedback loop: no listener, no adaptation, no goal-achievement monitoring. The controller isn't dead—it's running open-loop.



The full activation model:



A = β₁L + β₂H(Z)_ling + β₃H(Z)_comm + β₄(L × H(Z)_comm)



Where L is linguistic complexity, H(Z)_ling is linguistic surprisal (word predictability), and H(Z)_comm is communicative uncertainty—uncertainty about listener state, comprehension, and adaptation needs.



Fedorenko's paradigm measures the first two terms but sets H(Z)_comm = 0 by design. She studies the controller, but never lets it close the loop.



5.4 What She Measures vs. What Real Communication Requires



Consider these scenarios with identical linguistic complexity:



Fedorenko's paradigm: Read "The doctor examined the patient" in isolation. H(Z)_comm = 0 (no listener to model).



Real communication: Explain a medical condition to your anxious grandmother. H(Z)_comm is high—you're actively modeling her comprehension, watching for confusion, ready to rephrase.



Same linguistic content. But the second condition engages the communicative control system—the part that asks: Is she following? Should I simplify? Did that analogy land? This is the activation Fedorenko's paradigm cannot capture.



5.5 Reinterpreting Her Findings



Fedorenko's core findings remain valid—they just support a different conclusion:



"Surprisal modulates activation" → Yes, her own lab proved this. The linguistic uncertainty term H(Z)_ling is real and measurable.



"Language-selective" → Optimized for controlling linguistic signals, our primary communicative channel. Domain-specificity is expected for specialized controllers.



"Not needed for thought" → Controllers aren't needed for planning, only for execution toward a target. You don't need a thermostat to decide what temperature you want.



"Separate from Theory of Mind" → Yes, but synchronized with it. Fedorenko's work shows the language and ToM networks are functionally connected during naturalistic comprehension (Paunov et al., 2019). The controller (language) and the listener-model (ToM) are distinct modules that coordinate during closed-loop communication.



5.6 Language and Scale-Free Intelligence



Fedorenko found a controller. She just doesn't know it because she's never closed the loop.



This reframing actually strengthens the scale-free intelligence thesis. Language is not doing cognitive heavy lifting—it's a specialized controller for managing communicative uncertainty. Intelligence is fundamentally non-linguistic; language is an evolutionary innovation for sharing predictions across individual Markov blankets. The control loop just hasn't been studied with the loop closed.







VI. The Controllosphere Situates Cognitive Effort in Neural Energetics



Clay Holroyd's controllosphere concept, developed in his 2024 Psychological Review paper, provides a neural implementation account of how effortful control relates to free energy minimization (Holroyd, 2024).



6.1 The Architecture



The framework distinguishes between:



Intrinsic manifold: A low-dimensional, energy-efficient subspace of neural state space where automatic processing resides. This represents the "default" state of the system.



Controllosphere: An energy-inefficient region accessed when controlled processing is required. Transitions here are metabolically costly.



The architecture involves:



DLPFC (dorsolateral prefrontal cortex) as controller, driving system states into the controllosphere



ACC (anterior cingulate cortex) as value estimator, computing expected cost/benefit of controlled processing



PFC (broader prefrontal regions) implementing controlled processing when recruited



6.2 Cognitive Effort as Metabolic Cost



The key insight: cognitive effort just is the metabolic cost of maintaining neural states outside the intrinsic manifold. This is not metaphor—it is literal energetics. The "effort" of concentration, the "strain" of difficult thought, reflects actual metabolic expenditure required to maintain neural configurations that are energetically inefficient but computationally useful.



6.3 Free Energy Connection



Within the free energy framework, the controllosphere implements precision modulation. Controlled processing corresponds to increasing the precision (inverse variance) of prediction errors in task-relevant pathways, which requires additional metabolic investment but enables flexible, context-sensitive processing.



E_effort = ∫ (γ_controlled - γ_default) · metabolic_rate dt



Where γ represents precision weights and the integral captures cumulative metabolic cost over time.







VII. Nested Markov Blankets Enable Scale-Free Intelligence



The architecture that enables intelligence to scale from cells to societies is the nested Markov blanket—hierarchically organized statistical boundaries where each level both constitutes and constrains the levels above and below.



7.1 The Formal Structure



Formally, nested blankets arise when the internal states of one Markov blanket themselves decompose into further blanket structures:



μ = (μ₁, μ₂, ..., μₙ)



Where each μᵢ has its own blanket bᵢ = (sᵢ, aᵢ).



This nesting can continue recursively, generating hierarchies from molecular to cellular to organ to organism to social levels.



Palacios et al.'s 2020 Journal of Theoretical Biology paper "On Markov blankets and hierarchical self-organisation" provides the rigorous mathematical treatment, proving that hierarchical self-organization necessarily emerges in systems with nested blanket structure (Palacios, Razi, Parr, Kirchhoff, & Friston, 2020).



7.2 Empirical Instantiation



The nested architecture appears across biological scales:



Subcellular: Organelles maintain functional boundaries within cells.



Cellular: Individual cells maintain membrane-bounded internal states while participating in tissue-level dynamics.



Tissue: Epithelial sheets, neural networks, bioelectric networks maintain tissue-level organization while composed of individual cells.



Organ: Organs maintain functional boundaries while composed of multiple tissue types.



Organism: The body maintains its boundaries while composed of organ systems.



Social: Social groups, institutions, societies maintain collective organization while composed of individual organisms.



7.3 Information Flow



The key property: information flows both up and down the hierarchy. Lower-level dynamics provide the substrate that implements higher-level organization; higher-level organization constrains and coordinates lower-level dynamics.



This bidirectional flow is what distinguishes nested Markov blankets from simple aggregation. A pile of sand is an aggregate; a cell is a nested system. The difference is that in nested systems, higher-level organization actively constrains lower-level dynamics in ways that maintain the organization itself.







VIII. The Configuration Constraint Predicts Emergent Mental States



A key theoretical prediction emerges from the synthesis: the capacity for mental states emerges specifically from configurations of nested blankets, not from any particular substrate. This "configuration constraint" generates testable predictions that distinguish the framework from both classical cognitivism and simple emergentism.



8.1 The Core Prediction



The claim: mental states (beliefs, desires, experiences) emerge when:



A system maintains nested Markov blanket organization



The system's generative model includes representations of its own blanket structure



The hierarchical depth exceeds some threshold enabling self-referential inference



This predicts that mentality is substrate-independent but organization-dependent.



8.2 The Simulated Annealing Metaphor



The framework can be understood through the lens of simulated annealing, the optimization technique inspired by metallurgical annealing (Kirkpatrick, Gelatt, & Vecchi, 1983).



In cognitive/developmental annealing:



High plasticity: Young systems explore many organizational configurations



Maturation: System settles into stable functional configurations



Final state: Characteristic adult organization



The "configuration" refers to the specific nested blanket structure that emerges through this developmental annealing process.



8.3 Distinguishing Predictions



The configuration constraint generates predictions distinguishing it from competitor theories:



vs. Classical cognitivism: Mental states do not require symbol manipulation; they require appropriate organizational configuration.



vs. Simple emergentism: Not just "more is different"—specific organizational properties are necessary.



vs. Functionalism: Not just input-output relations but the internal nested structure matters.







IX. The Synthesis: From Cellular Inference to Conscious Experience



Drawing together the preceding threads, we can now articulate the unified model in its full scope.



9.1 The Core Architecture



Level 0 – Physics: Random dynamical systems governed by Langevin dynamics naturally partition into Markov blanket structures when they maintain themselves at nonequilibrium steady-state.



Level 1 – Life: Living systems are characterized by autopoietic organization—operationally closed networks of processes that produce themselves and their boundaries.



Level 2 – Cellular intelligence: Cells maintain sophisticated inference machinery, implementing Bayesian inference over their local environments.



Level 3 – Homeostatic regulation: Multicellular organisms develop hierarchical regulatory systems that maintain viability through continuous inference about body states.



Level 4 – Neural cognition: Nervous systems implement rapid, flexible inference through specialized computational architectures.



Level 5 – Self-modeling: Sufficiently complex systems develop generative models that include representations of their own Markov blanket structure.



Level 6 – Social cognition: Individual Markov blankets can be nested within higher-level social blankets, enabling collective inference and shared intentionality.



9.2 Illuminating Phenomena



This architecture illuminates several otherwise puzzling phenomena:



Language can be understood as a specialized control system that enables organisms to share predictions across individual Markov blankets—what might be formalized as minimizing the entropy of communicative outcomes. The language network is a communicative uncertainty controller, though typically studied under open-loop conditions that eliminate the feedback it evolved to manage.



Neural modules can be reframed as "frozen solutions to recurring regulatory demands"—stable patterns of organization that emerge through repeated minimization of the same free energy landscapes.



Consciousness can be characterized as "what self-modeling feels like from inside"—the phenomenal character that accompanies systems with sufficiently deep hierarchies of self-referential inference.







X. Critical Perspectives and Open Questions



10.1 The Emperor's New Markov Blankets



The most systematic critique appears in Bruineberg and colleagues' 2022 Behavioral and Brain Sciences paper "The Emperor's New Markov Blankets" (Bruineberg et al., 2022). The core objection distinguishes Pearl blankets from Friston blankets. As addressed in Section I.4, this critique assumes a sharp map/territory distinction that may itself be problematic within the FEP framework.



10.2 The Unfalsifiability Critique



Friston himself acknowledges that FEP is a principle, not an empirical hypothesis: "Like Hamilton's principle of stationary action, it cannot be falsified." The defense is that FEP generates testable process theories (predictive coding, active inference) even if the principle itself transcends empirical test.



10.3 The Hard Problem Reconsidered



The hard problem of consciousness—why functional organization should be accompanied by phenomenal experience—assumes that physical processes and phenomenal experience are distinct explananda. The framework developed here challenges this assumption. If feelings just ARE what self-referential interface modeling IS at sufficient complexity, then there is no gap requiring a bridging explanation.



Critics will object that this merely relocates the mystery: why should self-referential interface modeling feel like anything? But this objection presupposes the very framework being rejected. The question assumes that "feeling like something" is an additional property beyond the structural and dynamical properties of the system. The Damasio-informed position denies this: phenomenal character is not an additional property but rather what certain structural-dynamical configurations ARE from inside.



This is not eliminativism—we are not denying that feelings exist or that they have phenomenal character. We are denying that phenomenal character requires explanation in terms of something more fundamental. The redness of red, the painfulness of pain, the loneliness of loneliness—these are not mysteries to be solved but features to be mapped. The question becomes empirical: which interface configurations produce which phenomenal signatures, and how do they compose into the rich texture of conscious experience?



Whether this constitutes a solution to the hard problem or a rejection of it depends on one's philosophical commitments. What it offers is a research program: study the nested Markov blanket interfaces, map their phenomenal correlates, and trace how lower-level feelings converge into higher-level experience. If this program succeeds, the hard problem will not have been answered so much as dissolved—revealed as an artifact of a dualistic framing that the biology of feeling renders obsolete.



10.4 The Nativism Question



Fedorenko's acknowledgment that she cannot explain why experience-dependent development produces consistent localization suggests fundamental gaps in current understanding. The open-loop critique developed here suggests that her paradigm may be systematically missing the closed-loop communicative control dynamics that shaped the evolution of the language network.







XI. Synthesis and Future Directions



11.1 What Has Been Achieved



The synthesis presented here proposes that intelligence is not a special property emerging at some threshold of neural complexity but rather a fundamental feature of self-organizing systems that maintain statistical boundaries with their environments.



The specific contributions include:



Mathematical unification: Free energy minimization provides a common formal framework.



Scale-free architecture: Nested Markov blankets enable the same computational principles to operate at multiple scales.



Phenomenological grounding: Damasio's homeostatic framework grounds the mathematical formalism in embodied experience.



Interface phenomenology: Feelings arise not from prediction errors within a single system but from the dynamics at the interface between Markov blanketed systems. Lower-level feelings converge into higher-level ones through nested interface integration.



Hard problem dissolution: The "hard problem" is rejected as ill-posed. Phenomenal character is not an additional property produced by physical processes but rather what self-referential interface modeling IS at sufficient complexity. The question becomes empirical—mapping which configurations produce which phenomenal signatures—rather than metaphysical.



Communicative control: Language is reframed as a specialized uncertainty controller operating open-loop in standard experimental paradigms but closed-loop in natural communication.



11.2 What Remains Unresolved



The standards for attributing cognition across biological scales require refinement.



The mechanisms by which experience-dependent development produces consistent neural organization demand explanation.



The closed-loop dynamics of communicative control remain largely unstudied with appropriate paradigms.



Yet the convergence of mathematical physics, developmental biology, neuroscience, and philosophy on shared principles represents significant theoretical progress. Intelligence, on this account, is not a mysterious addition to the physical world but an inevitable consequence of the statistical mechanics of persistence—a principle that applies wherever distinguishable entities maintain themselves against thermodynamic dissolution.
